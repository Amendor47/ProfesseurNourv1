Four rules the whole game obeys.

Every system, line, and pixel is checked against these.

Love is not understanding

It's patient translation. Elias never fully gets Lou — the game is about staying through the not-getting.

02

Every mechanic is a metaphor

The race is keeping pace. The fight is intrusive thoughts. The quiz is misreading her. No system is just a system.

01 CONCEPT & LOGLINE

When you call me, singe.

The name he hates, that he answers to anyway.

Logline. When You Call Me, Singe is a bilingual interactive love story in which a quiet Swedish viewer, hopelessly in love, tries to learn the emotional language of Loupiote — a hot-tempered French-Maghrebi streamer who likes him, teases him, calls him "singe" (monkey)… but never loves him back quite as hard.

It plays as a visual novel braided with short arcade minigames: rooftop races, fistfights against intrusive thoughts, an emotion-decoding quiz, a delete-before-you-send text duel, and a final ringing phone. A single connexion meter — visualised as her warm palette bleeding into his cold one — tracks how close they actually get, and routes the story to one of three honest endings. Not soulmates. Not destiny. Just two people, badly matched, deciding night after night whether to stay on the call.

The title, decoded

"Singe" is Lou's pet name for Elias. He doesn't like it — it makes him feel like her amusement, not her equal. He answers to it anyway. The whole game lives in that gap: the things we accept from people who love us less than we love them.

02 GENRE & PLATFORM

A love story that won't sit in one genre.

Because the relationship doesn't either.

Choice-driven visual novel as the spine, with diegetic arcade interludes — each genre-shift mirrors an emotional gear-change.

Texture

Lo-fi / CRT

Pixel runners, scanlines, emoji iconography, WebAudio blips — the warm static of late-night internet intimacy.

Feel

Emotional-translation sim

Scope

05 WORLD & SETTING

Two climates, mostly lived through the wires.

The relationship happens online. Reality is the rare, terrifying intrusion.

Lou's world — warm

Always on, always loud

A cramped, glowing bedroom-studio. Stream overlays, Discord pings, chat scrolling like weather, 2 a.m. playlists, French slang, frog emotes. Affection performed at volume. Safe because it's a stage.

Elias's world — cold

Quiet, spacious, snowed-in

A Swedish winter: empty rooms, long light, frozen lakes, Stockholm looking "emotionally unavailable." Silence as a default, not a punishment. Terrifying to Lou, who fills it with noise.

Most of the love lives in liminal digital space — Discord calls, Twitch chat, a text thread, a buzzing Nokia. When Lou finally visits Sweden in Act II, the absence of an audience is the whole drama: no chat to perform for, no mute button on a silence, just two people and the weather. The warm and cold palettes are not decoration — they are the two emotional atmospheres, and the game's central image is how far they're willing to bleed into one another.

Bilingual UI

French in dusty pink, Swedish in lavender, English in cream. The colour is the emotional register — and which language she uses tells you how guarded she is.

three languages, one she trusts least: sincerity.

The Stream Layer — the streaming world, made mechanical

Lou's audience isn't backdrop; it's a second force pulling against Elias. During live scenes the chat scrolls beside the dialogue, reacting to everything — and the game runs two meters in opposition:

▲ AUDIENCE

Perform · Loupiote

Stay in persona, play to chat, overshare the drama. Instant love: sub alerts, emote spam, dopamine. Costs nothing now — and quietly tells Elias he's watching a show.

VS
▼ CONNEXION

Reveal · Lou

Break character, go quiet, take it to DMs. No applause, no reward you can see. The only person who notices is the one keeping the real meter.

Why it matters mechanically

Many beats let Lou feed only one meter at the other's expense — the dramatic engine of a streamer in love. Audience love is loud, immediate, and addictive; connexion is silent and slow. In Ch.4 the systems collide: oversharing the fight on stream spikes Audience and craters Connexion in the same click. The hidden 9-hour VOD (via Baudelaire) is the one place both meters go quiet and you simply watch her be a person.

09 SOUND & MUSIC

Two themes that learn to share instruments.

And silence treated as a playable sound.

Lou's theme — warm

Lo-fi & chanson

Boom-bap drums, a dusty Rhodes, a French vocal sample looped too long. Expressive, a little messy, always on.

Elias's theme — cold

Nordic ambient

Sparse piano, room tone, a single held synth. Minimal to the point of absence. Mostly the space between notes.

As connexion rises, the two scores interleave — her drums creep under his piano; his held note surfaces in her loop. Full blend only plays at the hopeful ending. The mix is the relationship.

Diegetic

The internet, heard

Discord join-hum, mechanical keyboard clicks, the Twitch alert sting, a Nokia ringtone. The sounds of being online together.

Signature

Dangerous notifications

One alert sound recurs at every emotional spike — so that by the bittersweet ending, the player flinches at it too. Sound as scar tissue.

Negative space

Silence is a track

The 3-hour call and the frozen-lake pause are scored with near-nothing — breathing, keyboard, wind. Restraint as intimacy.

10 SAMPLE WALKTHROUGH

One full run, beat by beat.

Every key scene, with the emotional + mechanical job each one does.

Landing

The buzzing Nokia

Title types itself; a Nokia rings with a French number and one word — "singe." Chat HUD shows Lou live and EliasNordicBlue following. The cat Baudelaire sleeps.

Job establish the world in one screen; plant the nickname and the hidden VOD before a word is spoken.

Ch.1 · contact

One blue heart

Elias watches Lou cry over a game. Chat spams frogs and hearts. Choice: send a single restrained heart, or close the tab. The heart is the whole game in one click — she notices the quiet one.

Job first connexion gain; teach that restraint reads louder than noise here.

Ch.1 · runner

Race across the rooftops

"Loser admits feelings first." Lou auto-runs; you jump cables to keep pace with Elias's ghost. Then the monkeys swarm — punch back the "you're too much" gremlins.

Job first genre-shift; turn flirtation and self-doubt into something you physically do.

Ch.2 · decode

The voice note he can't read

Lou leaves a four-minute ramble. Four decode questions: "whatever lol" really means "this matters and I'm scared it doesn't to you." You'll miss some. That's the lesson.

Job make Elias's core struggle — reading emotion — into the literal verb of the scene.

Ch.2 · intimacy

Asleep on the call

Lou falls asleep mid-Discord-call; Elias stays connected, silent, three hours, working to her breathing. Nothing romantic is said. It's the closest they've been.

Job the quietest connexion spike in the game — intimacy as presence, not words.

Ch.3 · Sweden

The frozen lake

No audience now. Branch through Stockholm and the café; at the lake she asks if people only love versions of you. His long pause lands, for once, as care. A second race over the ice.

Job reframe his silence from rejection to caution; biggest mid-game connexion swing.

Ch.4 · rupture

The overshare & the text duel

A fight leaks onto her stream — Audience spikes, Connexion craters. Elias vanishes to regulate. In the duel you type three furious paragraphs, then delete them and send "i got scared. that's all it was."

Job collide the stream-layer meters; reward the words not sent. His line: "I stay even when I don't understand."

Finale · the call

Let it ring, or answer

The Nokia rings. Wait, and Lou's monologue spirals through fear and self-talk. Answer, and the palettes fully blend for a held second — then the meter picks one of three endings.

Job the patience theme becomes a button; the whole run is scored into a single choice.

Hidden

Baudelaire's 9-hour VOD

Click the sleeping cat on the landing screen and he stretches, unlocking an archive chapter: Lou, off-persona, unperformed. The only place both meters fall silent.

Job reward the curious; show the real Lou the stream never gets to see.

11 COMPARABLE TITLES

Where it sits on the shelf.

Borrowing from each — landing in a gap none of them fill.

relationship-as-minigames

Florence

Romance told through small mechanical metaphors. We take its "feelings-as-verbs" approach and add real failure, real imbalance, and a second person who loves less.

parasocial streamer sim

NEEDY STREAMER OVERDOSE

Meter-driven, multi-ending, online-identity horror-comedy. We share its streamer-and-audience tension but aim it outward — at one viewer who became real.

bilingual VN, cultural heart

Butterfly Soup

Proof that cultural specificity + humour + ache outsells genericism. Our French-Maghrebi / Swedish register-switching lives in this lineage.

text-interface nostalgia

Emily is Away

Romance through a chat window and the things you almost typed. Our text-duel's delete mechanic is a direct descendant, with stakes attached.

lo-fi, emotional labour

VA-11 Hall-A

Mood, music, and conversation over spectacle. Our CRT-lounge texture and "listening is the gameplay" ethos share its DNA.

literary, magical-realist tone

Kentucky Route Zero

The benchmark for treating a game like literature. We borrow its confidence that quiet, strange, sincere writing is the feature.

The gap we fill: none of these put a volatile, half-invested streamer and a quiet, all-in foreigner who can't read her in the same uneven relationship — and let the mechanics tell the truth about who loves whom more. That asymmetry, plus the bilingual register-switching and the arcade interludes, is the whole pitch.

"What if love is not perfect understanding?
What if it is patient translation?"— and what if you do all the translating?
